Effects of Wave Exposure on Changes in Kelp Diversity and Abundance

Jay Matsushiba, Ellery Hardy, Lucero Gonzalez, Emilie Sadler

Introduction

Kelp substantially alters habitat structure and plays a major role in the function of coastal ecosystems. Much like coral reefs, kelp forests and kelp beds are being lost due to ocean warming and marine heat waves. Broad-scale stressors are often used in climate models to predict changes in communities over time (Starko et al. 2019, Helmuth et al. 2014). However, changes in kelp diversity and abundance may also be dependent on fine-scale environmental factors. As a local factor, how does wave exposure affect changes in kelp diversity and abundance?

Methods

Figure 1. (a) Photo of site 114 from the original Parks Canada study in the 1990s. (b) Photo of site 114 taken during our 2019 study.

We re-surveyed 40 sites from a Parks Canada study conducted in the 1990s throughout Barkley Sound. We recorded the presence and absence of species from a list of species of interest. The list consisted of 17 species of kelp as well as Fucus distichus, Phyllospadix spp., Sargassum muticum, and Desmarestia spp. At each site, we also estimated the abundance of listed species that were present, as well as the abundance of the top five most abundant turf species. Sites were categorized based on the level of wave action, designated as either “Sheltered”, “Moderate” or “Exposed”.

Results

Figure 2. Spatial representation of the change in richness of habitat-forming species between 1993 and 2019.

Since the original study conducted in the 1990s, sheltered sites have lost more kelp species than exposed sites (linear model, p = 0.0221; Fig. 4). Barnacle height was used as a proxy for wave exposure as barnacles are able to survive higher in the intertidal zone with increased wave action. The dominance of specific turf species is compared with kelp richness in Figure 5. N. subarticulatum and Leathesia spp. presence is significantly correlated with fewer kelp species (p = 0.00263, p = 0.00551). The results support similar studies, finding that kelp and turf species have a competitive relationship (Fibee-dexter and Wernberg, 2018).

Discussion

By investigating a local factor, such as wave exposure, our study adds to the body of knowledge that describes the impacts of climate change on kelp communities. We observed that sheltered sites experienced the greatest decline in kelp species richness and abundance between 1993 and 2019. On average, exposed sites did not experience losses in richness or abundance. We propose that data on the presence and abundance of macroalgae should continue to be collected in Barkley Sound. More surveys, especially at exposed sites, will further expand on the role of exposed sites as refugia for kelp communities. Incorporating temperature data and better abundance metrics would also help to capture finer changes in distribution.
